CH 4

Analysis of Assets & Capital Sources

A complete framework for evaluating a company's investment decisions, financing strategy, and financial stability — all derived from the balance sheet.

The Big Picture

Every balance sheet tells two stories: what a company owns (assets) and how it paid for those assets (capital sources). Chapter 4 teaches you to read both stories — tracking changes over time, comparing proportions, tracing where money came from and where it went, and judging whether the company's financial structure is sound.

Think of it as a financial health check: Is the company investing wisely? Is it funding itself safely? Is it managing its short-term cash needs effectively?
4.1
Asset Analysis
Examine how assets change over time (horizontal) and what proportion each asset holds (vertical). Identify investment decisions and risk exposure.
4.2
Capital Analysis
Analyze how the company raises money — through debt or equity — and assess financial autonomy, leverage, and risk profile.
4.3
Sources & Uses of Funds
Trace where capital came from and where it was invested between two periods. Evaluate matching of fund duration with asset duration.
4.4
Financial Balance
Assess structural balance using Net Working Capital (NWC). Determine if long-term assets are properly funded by long-term capital.
4.5
Working Capital Requirement
Measure how much capital is needed to fund day-to-day operations. Driven by the cash gap between paying suppliers and collecting from customers.
Key Methods Used in This Chapter
Horizontal Analysis
Change = End − Beginning
% Change = Change / Beginning × 100
Compares figures across different time periods to identify trends and growth rates.
Vertical Analysis
% Share = Item Value / Total × 100
Shows each item as a proportion of the whole (total assets or total capital), revealing the structure.
Data source for ALL sections: The Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position), supported by macroeconomic and microeconomic context during the analysis period.
Learning Outcomes
  • Analyze the scale and structure of assets using horizontal and vertical methods
  • Analyze the scale and structure of capital sources (liabilities + equity)
  • Assess the mobilization and utilization of capital through sources & uses analysis
  • Evaluate the company's financial balance using Net Working Capital (NWC)
  • Analyze Working Capital Requirement (WCR) and recommend improvements
4.1

Asset Analysis

Evaluate a company's investment decisions by examining how assets change over time and what proportion each asset occupies.

4.1.1 — Analysis of Asset Fluctuations (Horizontal)

Purpose: To evaluate the trend of changes in asset items and assess the company's investment decisions over time.

Formulas
Absolute Change = End-of-Period Value − Beginning-of-Period Value

Relative Change (%) = Absolute Change / Beginning-of-Period Value × 100%
A positive absolute change means the asset grew; negative means it shrank. Both can be good or bad depending on context — always interpret in light of business strategy.
Interpretation Guide for Asset Changes

Short-term Financial InvestmentsCompany optimizing idle cash through short-term investingNot making effective use of excess cash
Accounts ReceivableHigher bad debt risk; capital tied up in uncollected amountsBetter receivables management, faster collection
InventoriesExpecting demand surge; but risk of obsolescence / overstockingMay face difficulty maintaining adequate stock levels
Fixed AssetsExpanding production capacity; new machinery or facilitiesDepreciation, disposals, or downsizing
Construction in ProgressInvesting in factories or development projectsProjects completed → moved to Fixed Assets, or suspended
Investment PropertyInvesting in real estate for rental / capital appreciationDisposed or converted to owner-occupied use
LT Financial InvestmentsExpanding to subsidiaries, associates, joint venturesWithdrawing capital, divesting, or revaluing portfolio

4.3

Analysis of Capital Sources & Uses of Funds

Trace where money came from and where it was deployed between two periods, then assess whether matching is appropriate.

Core Logic — What Counts as "Use" vs "Source"?
📤 USE of Funds (Capital Deployed)
Increase in Assets
You spent money to grow assets (e.g., bought more inventory)
Decrease in Liabilities
You used cash to repay debt (e.g., paid off bank loan)
Decrease in Equity
Paid dividends, bought back shares, or incurred losses
📥 SOURCE of Funds (Capital Raised)
Decrease in Assets
You converted assets to cash (e.g., collected receivables)
Increase in Liabilities
You borrowed or extended payables (e.g., took bank loan)
Increase in Equity
Raised equity capital or retained profits
Accounting identity check: Total Uses of Funds = Total Sources of Funds (always balances, just like the balance sheet).
4-Step Process for Sources & Uses Analysis
1

Condense the Balance Sheet

Group assets and capital items with similar characteristics into meaningful categories (e.g., combine all current liabilities, all equity items). Purpose: highlight key structural components clearly.

2

Prepare the Statement of Capital Sources & Uses

Compare end-of-period vs beginning-of-period for each line item. Record each change as either a Use or a Source following the rules above. Verify total Uses = total Sources.

3

Prepare the Analysis Table

Group all Uses into: (I) Increase in Assets, (II) Decrease in Liabilities, (III) Decrease in Equity. Group all Sources into: (I) Decrease in Assets, (II) Increase in Liabilities, (III) Increase in Equity. Calculate percentage share of each group.

4

Interpretation & Evaluation — 3 Dimensions

① Fund Utilization: What was the primary capital need? Was the use reasonable?
② Capital Sources: What were the main sources? Short-term or long-term? Internal or external?
③ Matching Analysis: Are long-term assets funded by long-term capital? Use the Equity Ratio, LTD-to-TA, and Equity Multiplier.

Key Ratios for Matching Analysis
Equity Ratio
= Total Equity / Total Assets
Measures the share of assets financed by shareholders. Higher = greater financial autonomy. TAN: 92.24% (2022) → 96.60% (2023)
LT Debt to Total Assets
= Long-term Debt / Total Assets
Measures reliance on long-term external debt. TAN: extremely low as almost no LT debt.
Equity Multiplier
= Total Assets / Total Equity
Shows how much total assets each VND of equity supports. Higher = more leveraged. TAN: ~1.08 (2022) → ~1.04 (2023) — very low leverage.
Interpretation principle: Long-term assets should be funded by long-term capital (LT debt + equity). If short-term funds are used for long-term assets, the financial structure is risky and imbalanced.
4.4

Financial Balance Analysis

Assess whether the company's long-term assets are properly funded by long-term capital, using Net Working Capital (NWC).

Defining Net Working Capital (NWC)
Two equivalent formulas for NWC
NWC = Long-term Capital (LTC) − Long-term Assets (LTA)

NWC = Current Assets (CA) − Current Liabilities (CL)

Where: Long-term Capital = Long-term Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
NWC represents the portion of long-term capital left over after funding all long-term assets. That surplus is available to help fund current assets — providing a buffer against short-term financial stress.
ASSETS
Current Assets (CA)
Non-current Assets (NCA = LTA)
LIABILITIES & EQUITY
Current Liabilities (CL = ST Capital)
Non-current Liabilities
Shareholders' Equity
▲ NWC = LTC − LTA = CA − CL
4.4.1 — Three States of NWC
Case 1: NWC > 0
LTC > LTA
✅ Balanced. All long-term assets funded by long-term capital. Remaining LTC also helps fund current assets → financial structure is stable.
Case 2: NWC < 0
LTC < LTA
❌ Imbalanced. Long-term capital is insufficient to fund all LT assets. Short-term funds are being used for long-term purposes → risky financial structure.
Case 3: NWC = 0
LTC = LTA
⚠️ Fragile balance. LT capital just covers LT assets. No buffer for current assets. Appears balanced but is vulnerable to any disruption.
4.4.2 — How to Analyze Financial Balance
①

Identify the NWC sign (positive / negative / zero)

This tells you immediately whether the financial structure is balanced. Compute NWC = LTC − LTA or CA − CL for each period.

②

If NWC > 0: Assess the mix of LTC vs ST capital in funding CA

LT capital used to finance CA = (LTC − LTA) / CA
ST capital used to finance CA = CL / CA
Higher LT proportion = lower risk but higher capital cost.

③

If NWC < 0: Assess the severity of the imbalance

LT capital used to finance LTA = (LTC − LTA) / LTA (will be negative)
The more negative this ratio, the more severe the imbalance — a higher proportion of LT assets is funded by short-term capital.

4.5

Analysis of Working Capital Requirement (WCR)

Measure the funding needed to sustain day-to-day operations — driven by timing gaps between paying suppliers and collecting from customers.

4.5.1 — Definition & Formula
Working Capital Requirement
WCR = Current Assets (CA) − Short-term Operating Liabilities (STOL)

STOL = Trade payables, customer advances, accrued wages, taxes payable — operating payables that do NOT include bank borrowings
WCR arises from the cash gap: the time between cash outflows (paying for inputs) and cash inflows (collecting from customers). The longer the gap, the more working capital is needed.
Intuitive Example

Imagine a garment factory: it must pay for fabric and labor today, but the retailer only pays after 60 days. The factory needs capital to cover those 60 days of operating costs before cash flows back in. That need is the WCR.

If suppliers give 30-day credit: net cash gap = 60 − 30 = 30 days. The company must self-finance 30 days of operations.

Key Drivers of WCR
WCR increases when...
Inventories rise (longer storage time) · Receivables increase (slower collections) · Payables fall (paying suppliers faster) · Business scale expands
WCR decreases when...
Inventories fall (faster turnover) · Receivables fall (faster collections) · Payables rise (longer supplier credit) · Business scale contracts
4.5.2 — Analysis Steps
1

Identify Changes in WCR, CA, and STOL

Compute WCR for each period. Calculate the absolute and percentage change in WCR, CA, and STOL between beginning and end of period.

2

Determine How CA Changes Affect WCR

If CA increases, WCR rises (more capital needed). Break down CA change by component: which asset drove the increase — inventory, receivables, or other? Assess whether the increase is justified.

3

Determine How STOL Changes Affect WCR

If STOL increases, WCR falls (suppliers or customers are effectively financing operations). Assess whether the increase came from supplier credit extension or customer advances.

4

Summarize & Recommend

Identify the primary drivers of WCR change. Propose solutions: speed up collections, negotiate longer supplier terms, reduce excess inventory, or restructure financing mix.

NWC vs WCR — What's the Difference?
Net Working Capital (NWC)
NWC = LTC − LTA = CA − CL
Measures the structural financial balance. Shows whether long-term assets are safely funded by long-term capital. A strategic / structural concept.
Working Capital Requirement (WCR)
WCR = CA − STOL
Measures the operational cash need. Shows how much capital is needed to fund daily business operations and bridge the cash timing gap. An operational concept.
Key distinction: NWC uses all current liabilities (CL) in its formula, while WCR uses only operating current liabilities (STOL), excluding short-term bank borrowings. WCR is more focused on operating cycle management.
