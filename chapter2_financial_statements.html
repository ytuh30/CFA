2.1 Overview of Financial Statements

Definition · Significance · Classification · Advantages & Disadvantages

Definition: Financial statements are reports prepared according to current accounting standards and regulations, reflecting a comprehensive overview of assets, equity, liabilities, cash flows, as well as the financial situation and business results during the period of the enterprise.

Who Uses Financial Statements?

🏢 Business: Overview of financial situation → managers make accurate decisions → sustainable development
🏦 Banks: Assess operational status & debt repayment ability → decide loan amount & duration
📦 Suppliers: Assess liquidity → decide whether to allow buy-now-pay-later terms
💰 Investors: Judge business health → decide to invest or divest
🏛 Government: Verify correct tax payment amount & adjust/change policies

Classification of Financial Statements

By Content Reflected

  • Balance Sheet (BS) — Assets & Sources (Liabilities + Equity)
  • Income Statement (IS) — Sales & Expenses → Profit
  • Cash Flow Statement (CFS) — Cash In & Cash Out
  • Notes to Financial Statements — Explanatory information

By Preparation Time

  • Annual — for one full financial year
  • Interim — monthly or quarterly (midway through year)

By Scope

  • Consolidated: Parent company + subsidiaries (entire group) — summarizes financial situation of whole group
  • Separate: Reflects the parent company alone — financial situation of parent only

Advantages vs Disadvantages

✅ Comprehensive overview; Useful for management; Helps stakeholders decide
❌ Objectivity questionable; Data is historical; Differences in accounting methods applied

2.2 Balance Sheet (BS)

A snapshot of the company's financial position at a SPECIFIC POINT IN TIME

Definition: The Balance Sheet reflects the total value of existing assets and the sources that form those assets of an enterprise at a specific point in time (e.g., December 31 of a year).
Assets (A) = Liabilities (L) + Owner's Equity (E)
The Fundamental Accounting Equation — Always must balance!
All indicators expressed in monetary terms
Divided into two parts: Asset structure & Sources of assets
Reflects capital at a specific point in time, not a period

Significance of the Balance Sheet

Evaluates structure of assets and sources of capital
Assesses financial situation of the enterprise
Evaluates liquidity of the enterprise
Assets Side — Sorted by DECREASING Liquidity (most liquid first)

ASSETS

A. Current Assets — Expected to convert to cash within 1 year or 1 operating cycle. Higher liquidity.
  • Cash & Cash Equivalents — Currency, bank deposits, investments with original maturity ≤ 90 days
  • Short-term Financial Investments — Marketable securities and other investments expected to sell short-term
  • Accounts Receivable — Amounts due from customers from credit sales of products/services
  • Inventories — Goods purchased or produced for sale to customers
  • Other Current Assets — Prepaid expenses (rent, insurance, advertising paid in advance)
B. Non-current (Long-term) Assets — Expected to be held for more than 1 year. Provides info about firm's investing activities.
  • Long-term Receivables — Amounts due after 1 year
  • Fixed Assets (PPE) — Land, factory buildings, warehouses, machinery, vehicles, office equipment
  • Investment Properties — Real estate held for rental income or capital appreciation
  • Long-term Financial Investments — Investments not intended to sell in near future
  • Intangible & Other Assets — Patents, trademarks, goodwill, franchise rights (no physical substance)
  • Construction in Progress — Assets not yet placed into service
CriteriaCurrent AssetsLong-term Assets
Turnover TimeWithin 12 months or 1 operating cycleMore than 12 months
LiquidityHigherLower
Financing SourceCan use BOTH short-term and long-term capitalMust use ONLY long-term capital
Liabilities & Equity Side — Sorted by INCREASING Maturity (shortest first)

LIABILITIES & OWNER'S EQUITY

A. Liabilities — Current obligations from past transactions the business must settle from its resources. Sorting principle: by the term of the debt.

I. Current Liabilities (≤ 1 year or 1 operating cycle)

  • Short-term Borrowings & Finance Lease Liabilities — Short-term bank loans
  • Accounts Payable — Owed to suppliers for goods/services on credit
  • Accrued Liabilities — Incurred but not yet paid (wages, interest, taxes payable)
  • Unearned Revenues — Payment received before goods/services delivered
  • Current Maturities of LT Debt — Principal portion of LT debt due within 1 year

II. Non-current Liabilities (> 1 year)

  • Long-term Borrowings & Finance Lease Liabilities — Bonds, mortgages, long-term loans
  • Long-term Payables — Various obligations settled beyond 1 year
  • Deferred Tax Liabilities — Future tax obligations from temporary differences
B. Owner's Equity — Value calculated as Assets minus Liabilities. Residual interest of owners. No fixed maturity or interest obligation.
  • Owner's Capital Contribution — Capital directly invested by owners/shareholders
  • Common Stock — Par value from original sale of common stock to investors
  • Capital Surplus (Additional Paid-in Capital) — Amounts received above par value
  • Preferred Stock — Fewer ownership rights than common stock
  • Treasury Stock — Amount paid to reacquire own stock (deducted from equity)
  • Retained Earnings / Undistributed Profits — Accumulated net income not paid as dividends
  • Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) — Fair value changes not in income statement
RE(end) = RE(begin) + Net Income − Dividends
Retained Earnings bridge formula
CriteriaLiabilitiesOwner's Equity
SourceCreditors (external)Owners (internal)
Interest PaymentMust pay interestNo interest obligation
MaturityFixed maturity dateNo maturity
CollateralRequiredNot required
Tax ShieldTax-deductible (interest)Not tax-deductible
Cost of CapitalLower (tax shield)Higher (more risk)

⚠️ Limitations of the Balance Sheet

Book Values Only: Values are historical book values — may not reflect current market values
Accuracy Issues: Some items included (estimates, provisions) may not be accurate
Historical Situation: Only reflects the past; doesn't capture future potential or risks
Point-in-time Snapshot: Only a single moment — doesn't show trends or seasonal fluctuations
Omissions: Many important items are excluded from valuation (brand equity, human capital, customer relationships, innovation potential)

2.3 Income Statement (IS)

Reflects income, expenses, and results over a period of time — uses ACCRUAL basis

Definition: The Income Statement reflects the income, expenses, and business results of an enterprise over a period of time. It answers: "How did the company perform? Did it generate a profit or a loss? Did it create economic value?"

Three Activity Categories on the IS

Core Business Activities — Sales revenue, COGS, Selling Expenses, G&A Expenses → Core Business Profit
Financial Activities — Financial Income, Financial Expenses (including Interest) → Financial Profit
Other Activities — Non-routine transactions (fixed asset disposals, penalties, recoveries) → Other Profit

Two Key Accounting Principles

Revenue Recognition: Revenue recognized when transaction occurs, economic benefits are certain, and amount is determinable — regardless of whether cash has been received.
Expense Recognition (Matching Principle): Expenses recognized in the period when the related revenue is recognized — not when cash is paid.

📐 The Complete Income Statement Flow

① Gross Revenue (01) − Deductions (02) → Net Revenue (10 = 01−02)
Net Revenue (10) − COGS (11) → Gross Profit (20 = 10−11)
Gross Profit (20) + (21−22) Fin. Profit − Sell. (25) − G&A (26) → Net Op. Profit (30)
Net Op. Profit (30) + (31−32) Other Profit (40) → EBT / Profit Before Tax (50)
EBT (50) − ITE Current (51) − ITE Deferred (52) → EAT / Net Profit (60)
CBP = GP − Selling Expenses − G&A Expenses  |  EBIT = EBT + I  |  EAT = EBT − ITE
Core formulas for the exam
Key Income Statement Items

Revenue Deductions (Codes 02)

  • Trade Discounts — Given at time of sale to encourage bulk buying (e.g., 10% off for 1,000+ units) → reduces Net Revenue
  • Sales Reductions — Price reductions for defective or low-quality goods
  • Value of Returned Goods — Revenue reversed for goods returned by customers
Trade Discount (reduces revenue) ≠ Payment Discount (e.g., "2/10, net 30" → increases financial expenses)

COGS (Code 11) — Varies by Sector

  • Trade sector: Total purchase price + procurement costs of goods sold during the period
  • Service sector: Direct costs incurred to create services provided during the period
  • Manufacturing: Total production cost of the quantity of products sold during the period

Selling Expenses (Code 25)

  • Transportation costs, loading/unloading of goods
  • Salaries for sales and marketing staff
  • Agent commissions
  • Advertising expenses, promotions
  • Customer conference costs

General & Administrative Expenses (Code 26)

  • Salaries for management/administrative employees
  • Expenses for fixed assets used in management
  • Costs for outsourced services
  • Other overhead costs for management department

🔍 EBIT, EBT, and EAT — The Profit Cascade

EBIT (Earnings Before Interest & Taxes)
Profit before impact of financing decisions and taxes.
= CBP + Fin Profit (excl. I) + Other Profit
= EBT + Interest Expense (I)
EBT (Earnings Before Tax)
Overall results before corporate income tax.
= EBIT − Interest Expense
= CBP + Financial Profit + Other Profit
EAT (Earnings After Tax / Net Income)
The FINAL profit belonging to the owners.
= EBT − Income Tax Expense (ITE)

Income Tax — Temporary vs Permanent Differences

💰 Income Tax Expense (ITE)

ITE = ITE-Current + ITE-Deferred  |  ITE-Current = TI × t  |  ITE-Deferred = TD × t
TI = Taxable Income · t = Tax Rate · TD = Temporary Difference
Temporary Difference (TD) — Difference in TIMING between EBT and TI that WILL REVERSE in the future. Creates Deferred Tax items (DTA or DTL).

TD = EBT − TI
When TD exists: ITE = EBT × t and EAT = EBT × (1−t)
FeatureDTADTL
ConditionEBT < TI (pay more tax NOW)EBT > TI (pay less tax NOW)
Future ImpactFuture tax reductionFuture tax increase
Balance SheetAsset (non-current)Liability (non-current)
Permanent Difference (PD) — Difference between EBT and TI from revenues/expenses NEVER included in TI. NEVER REVERSES. Changes effective tax rate.

PD = |EBT − TI|
When PD exists: ITE ≠ EBT×t and EAT ≠ EBT×(1−t)
PD from Tax-Exempt Income: Income in EBT but NOT in TI (e.g., donations received, income from capital contributions). TI < EBT → Tax burden DECREASES → EAT > EBT×(1−t)
PD from Non-deductible Expenses: Expenses in EBT but NOT allowed for TI (e.g., no-invoice expenses, excess advertising costs, excess loan interest). TI > EBT → Tax burden INCREASES → EAT < EBT×(1−t)

📝 Example 2.5 — Tax-Exempt Income (PD)

Revenue = 100, Expenses = 70, Tax-exempt income = 10 (within revenue), Tax rate = 20%
  • EBT = 100 − 70 = 30
  • TI = (100−10) − 70 = 20 (exempt income excluded from TI)
  • PD = |30 − 20| = 10
  • ITE = 20 × 20% = 4 (based on TI, not EBT)
  • EAT = 30 − 4 = 26
  • EBT×(1−t) = 30×0.8 = 24; EAT(26) > 24 ✓ Tax reduced

📝 Example 2.6 — Non-deductible Expense (PD)

Revenue = 100, Expenses = 70 (incl. 10 non-deductible), Tax rate = 20%
  • EBT = 100 − 70 = 30
  • TI = 100 − (70−10) = 100 − 60 = 40 (non-deductible added back to TI)
  • PD = |30 − 40| = 10
  • ITE = 40 × 20% = 8
  • EAT = 30 − 8 = 22
  • EBT×(1−t) = 24; EAT(22) < 24 ✓ Tax burden increased

⚠️ Limitations of the Income Statement

Does NOT show actual cash inflows/outflows during the period
Accounting profit can be distorted by management accounting choices
Risk of "fake profit, real loss" — profitable on IS but negative cash flow in reality

2.4 Cash Flow Statement (CFS)

Uses CASH ACCOUNTING — recorded only when cash actually changes hands

Definition: The CFS shows where cash comes from (inflows) and where it goes (outflows) during a period. Divided into three main activities: Operating (CFO), Investing (CFI), and Financing (CFF).
Key Difference: BS and IS use ACCRUAL accounting — recorded when earned/incurred. CFS uses CASH accounting — recorded only when cash actually comes in or goes out.
Cash(end) = Cash(begin) + NCFO + NCFI + NCFF + Exchange Rate Effect
The fundamental cash balance equation
Three Cash Flow Components
🔄

CFO — Operating

Cash from main business activities. Reflects the company's ability to generate cash.
NCFO = (1)+(6) − (2)−(3)−(4)−(5)−(7)
(+) Cash received from customers
(+) Other operating receipts
(−) Paid to suppliers, employees
(−) Interest paid, Income tax paid
Interpretation
NCFO > 0Cash surplus
NCFO = 0Break even
NCFO < 0Cash deficit
🏗

CFI — Investing

Cash from fixed assets, real estate, financial investments. Reflects investment trends.
NCFI = (2)+(4)+(6)+(7) − (1)−(3)−(5)
(+) Disposal of fixed assets
(+) Receipts from term deposits
(+) Collections from investments
(+) Interest & dividends received
(−) Purchase of fixed assets, PPE
(−) Payments for investments
Interpretation
NCFI > 0Contracting
NCFI = 0No trend
NCFI < 0Expanding 📈
🏦

CFF — Financing

Cash flows changing borrowed capital and equity structure. Reflects external financing.
NCFF = (1)+(3) − (2)−(4)−(5)−(6)
(+) Proceeds from borrowings
(+) Capital contributions
(−) Loan principal repayment
(−) Dividends paid
(−) Capital refunded
Interpretation
NCFF > 0External financed
NCFF = 0No external need
NCFF < 0Repaid debt/dividends

🔄 Indirect Method for NCFO

The indirect method starts from EBT (or EAT) and adjusts for non-cash items and working capital changes to arrive at NCFO.
Starting from EBT:
  • 1. EBT
  • 2. Add back non-cash/non-operating items:
    + Depreciation & Amortization
    ± Provisions (+ increase, − decrease)
    − Gain / + Loss from investing activities
    + Interest Expense (added back here)
  • 3. Operating Profit before WC changes
  • ± Changes in receivables (↑ = outflow)
  • ± Changes in inventories (↑ = outflow)
  • ± Changes in payables excl. interest & CIT (↑ = inflow)
  • ± Changes in prepaid expenses
  • − Interest paid (separately deducted)
  • − Income tax paid (separately deducted)
  • = NCFO
Starting from EAT:
  • 1. EAT
  • 2. Add back non-cash items:
    + Depreciation & Amortization
    ± Provisions
    − Gain / + Loss from investing
    (Note: Interest expense NOT added back — already in EAT)
  • 3. Cash Flow before WC changes
  • ± Changes in WC (ΔWC)
    Includes changes in payables for interest and tax payable
  • 4. Cash Flow after WC changes
  • ± Other factors
  • = NCFO
Key Difference: EBT method adds back interest expense and separately deducts cash paid for interest and tax. EAT method includes these effects within working capital changes (via changes in interest payable and taxes payable).

⚠️ Limitations of the Cash Flow Statement

Does NOT reflect the enterprise's profit for the reporting period
Does NOT present the financial position (assets, liabilities, receivables, payables) at period end

2.5 Notes to the Financial Statements

Explanatory documents that help users better understand BS, IS, and CFS

Definition: Notes to the Financial Statements are explanatory documents that help users better understand the Balance Sheet, the Income Statement, and the Cash Flow Statement. They provide context, accounting policies, and additional detail.

📌 Significance

  • Provides information on the basis used to prepare the financial statements and specific accounting policies selected and applied to significant transactions and events
  • Provides information required by accounting standards that has not yet been presented in the other financial statements
  • Provides additional information not in other statements but necessary for a true and fair presentation of the enterprise's financial situation

📋 Information Included in Notes

  • Nature of the company's operations (business description)
  • Accounting period and the currency used
  • Accounting standards and accounting regulations applied in preparing the financial statements
  • Applied accounting policies (depreciation method, inventory valuation, revenue recognition criteria, etc.)
  • Additional information supporting the Balance Sheet, Income Statement, and Cash Flow Statement (detailed breakdowns, contingencies, commitments)

🔗 Relationships Between All Three Financial Statements

IS → BS: Net Income flows into Retained Earnings (equity). Revenue recognition creates Accounts Receivable. Accrued expenses create Accrued Liabilities.
BS → CFS: Changes in BS items drive Working Capital adjustments in indirect method. Cash balance on CFS = Cash on BS. Non-cash assets explain gap between profit and cash.
IS → CFS: Net Income is the starting point for indirect method CFS. Depreciation (IS expense) added back to CFS. Non-cash revenues subtracted. Timing differences create the gap.
