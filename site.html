§ 4.1 — The Asset Side

Asset Analysis: Where Did the Money Go?

Every dollar on the asset side of the balance sheet represents a decision someone already made — to hold cash, to let customers buy on credit, to stock inventory, to buy a machine. Asset analysis is the act of reading those decisions back out of the numbers.

4.1.1

Asset Fluctuation Analysis (Horizontal Analysis)

Horizontal analysis tracks one asset item across time — month to month, year to year — and computes how much it changed in absolute terms (e.g. +16,860 million VND) and in relative terms (e.g. +8.4%). It answers "how is this number moving?", not "how big is it right now?"

  • Managers use it to check whether investment decisions (buying equipment, building inventory, extending credit) are happening as planned and at the right pace.
  • Creditors watch it to see whether the assets backing their loan are growing, shrinking, or quietly being converted into something less liquid (e.g. cash turning into receivables that may not be collected).
  • Investors read fluctuation as a proxy for strategy in motion — expansion, contraction, or a pivot in business model.

An asset cannot grow on its own — every increase in assets had to be financed by something (more debt, more equity, retained profit, or a decrease somewhere else). This is the seed of §4.3: asset fluctuation is simply "uses of funds" viewed item-by-item. So before judging an increase as good or bad, always ask the matching question: what financed it, and is that financing appropriate for that asset's time horizon?

No item is "good up, bad down" by itself — direction only becomes meaningful with context:

Item↑ Increase usually means↓ Decrease usually means
Cash & equivalentsLiquidity improves — but idle cash earns little; may signal underinvestmentTighter liquidity — but could mean cash is being put to work
ReceivablesLooser credit terms can drive sales, but capital gets tied up and bad-debt risk risesFaster collection / stricter policy — good for liquidity, but could also mean lost sales
InventoriesExpecting demand growth, or early signs of overstocking / obsolescenceLeaner operations, or risk of stock-outs that disappoint customers
Fixed assetsCapacity expansion, rising operating leverage, a bet on future demandDivestment, depreciation outpacing capex, or a shrinking operating base

Sustained, consistent asset growth funded by stable sources usually signals a healthy, expanding business. Erratic swings — large one-year jumps followed by reversals — usually signal weak forecasting, opportunistic cash management, or instability worth investigating before forming a conclusion about performance.

See the full worked case below — TAN Company, 2022–2024.

  • Reporting only the % change and ignoring the absolute amount (a 783% increase on a tiny base may be financially trivial).
  • Reporting only the absolute change and ignoring %, which hides how large the shift was relative to the item's own size.
  • Calling every increase "growth" and every decrease "decline" without asking which item moved and why.
  • Forgetting to connect the fluctuation back to its financing source (the §4.3 link).

4.1.2

Asset Structure Analysis (Vertical Analysis)

Vertical analysis expresses every asset item as a % of total assets at one point in time. Instead of "how did it change?", it answers "how is the pie sliced right now — and is the company over-concentrated in one slice?"

Structure reveals diversification and concentration risk. A company with 80% of its assets in a single receivable from one customer carries very different risk from one spread across cash, inventory, and equipment, even if both have the same total assets.

Asset structure is a fingerprint of the business model. A trading/distribution company will naturally carry high inventory and receivables and low fixed assets. A manufacturer or utility will carry heavy fixed assets. A bank will carry almost no inventory at all. There is no universally "correct" structure — only a structure that is, or is not, consistent with how the company actually earns revenue.

Combine the % weight (vertical) with how that weight is moving (horizontal). A current-assets share climbing from 80% to 82% while inventory's own share also climbs tells a more complete story than either number alone — together they suggest the company is becoming more working-capital intensive over time.

A current-asset-heavy structure usually means more liquidity and flexibility but lower long-run returns on capital. A fixed-asset-heavy structure usually means higher operating leverage — profit is more sensitive to revenue swings in both directions — and less flexibility to scale down quickly in a downturn.

TAN Company carries roughly 80–82% of total assets in current assets and only ~13% in fixed assets across 2022–2024 (see table below) — a structure typical of a trading or light-manufacturing business, not a capital-intensive one like a utility or heavy industrial firm.

  • Treating a high current-asset % as automatically "safe" — it can also mean capital is parked unproductively in receivables or excess inventory.
  • Comparing structure across two unrelated industries and calling one "better" without adjusting for business model.
  • Quoting only one year's % and forgetting to discuss the trend across the period.

Focus

Special emphasis: reading asset structure as strategy

⚖ Current vs. Non-current

Current assets are the company's liquidity reserve and operating fuel; non-current assets are its productive capacity. The balance between them is a direct trade-off between flexibility and scale of operation — and it sets up §4.4, where the time-matching of assets to capital determines financial stability.

🏭 Rising Fixed Assets

An increasing fixed-asset share signals capacity expansion and a strategic bet on future demand. It raises operating leverage: fixed costs (depreciation) stay constant regardless of sales volume, so profit becomes more volatile — a powerful upside in growth years, a painful downside in slow years.

📦 Rising Inventories

Can mean the company is positioning for expected demand growth (positive), or that goods aren't selling as planned and are piling up (negative). The differentiator is whether inventory growth is proportional to — or faster than — revenue growth, and whether it's matched by appropriate financing.

Exam tip

Examiners reward students who explicitly separate horizontal ("how is it changing?") from vertical ("how big a slice is it?") and then recombine them into one judgement. Never present the two tables without a synthesizing sentence.

Exam Prep

Essay questions on Asset Analysis

“Explain the purpose and method of asset structure analysis. Using a hypothetical company, discuss what a rising share of fixed assets in total assets might indicate about its strategy and risk.
Model answer outline

Purpose/Method (2–3 sentences): State that asset structure analysis uses vertical analysis to measure each asset's % of total assets, aiming to assess diversification and risk exposure; horizontal analysis is layered on to track how that structure evolves.

Application: If Company X's fixed-asset share rises from 20% to 35% over three years, this indicates a capacity-expansion strategy — likely investment in production lines, equipment, or facilities to support anticipated demand growth.

Risk discussion: Explain operating leverage — higher fixed costs (depreciation) mean profit becomes more sensitive to revenue changes. Link to financing: ask whether this fixed-asset growth was financed with long-term capital (appropriate, per §4.4) or short-term capital (a financing-mismatch risk).

Conclusion: A reasoned judgement, not a verdict — e.g. "the strategy is reasonable if demand forecasts hold and financing is long-term; otherwise it raises both operating and financial risk simultaneously."

“A company's inventories increased by 30% while sales grew only 8%. Is this a positive or negative signal? Justify your answer.
Model answer outline

Lead with the mismatch: inventory growth (30%) outpacing sales growth (8%) is a warning sign, not automatically negative, but it should be investigated. Possible explanations: (1) deliberate stock-build ahead of an expected demand surge — defensible if supported by forward orders; (2) slowing sell-through / overstocking — a sign of inefficient capital use and rising obsolescence/holding-cost risk. The correct exam answer evaluates both possibilities and states what further evidence (inventory turnover ratio, order backlog) would resolve the ambiguity, rather than picking one side without justification.

§ 4.2 — The Capital Side

Capital Analysis: Whose Money Is It, and What Does That Cost?

If asset analysis asks "where did the money go?", capital analysis asks the mirror question: where did the money come from, and what claim does that source hold over the company? Every source — supplier credit, a bank loan, a shareholder's investment — comes with a different price, a different deadline, and a different amount of control surrendered.

4.2.1

Capital Fluctuation Analysis (Horizontal)

The same horizontal logic as 4.1.1, applied to liabilities and equity: track each capital item's absolute and % change across periods to assess the company's capacity to mobilise financial resources.

Lenders care whether a company can keep attracting financing on reasonable terms. Shareholders care whether growth in equity comes from new share issues (dilution) or retained profit (value creation). A sudden spike in short-term borrowing, for instance, can foreshadow a liquidity squeeze well before it appears anywhere else.

Just as every asset increase needs financing, every capital increase needs a destination — it either funded new assets or replaced another capital source that shrank. Capital fluctuation is "sources of funds" viewed item-by-item, the other half of §4.3.

Item↑ Increase usually means↓ Decrease usually means
Accounts payableGreater reliance on supplier credit — can be efficient financing, or a sign of cash strainPaying suppliers faster — builds trust, but uses cash sooner
Short-term borrowingsFinancing a short-term need, or covering a liquidity gapDeleveraging — improves safety, frees future cash flow
Retained earningsThe company is profitable and reinvesting — strengthens internal financingLosses, or heavy dividend/reserve allocation — weakens reinvestment capacity
Shareholders' equityNew shares issued — more permanent capital, but ownership is dilutedShare buybacks or losses — concentrates ownership but may signal distress if loss-driven

Capital fluctuation patterns reveal financing strategy and resilience: a company that funds growth mainly through retained earnings is building durable, low-risk capacity; one that funds growth mainly through fresh short-term debt each year is building fragility.

  • Assuming all debt growth is bad and all equity growth is good — both depend on cost, terms, and use.
  • Forgetting retained earnings is itself a capital source — it doesn't "just sit there", it finances assets like any other source.
  • Ignoring the sign of retained earnings (negative accumulated losses look structurally different from positive retained profit) when computing % changes.

4.2.2

Capital Structure Analysis (Vertical)

Each liability and equity item expressed as a % of total capital. It is the classic debt-vs-equity question: how much of the company belongs, financially, to creditors versus owners?

It tells you (1) financial autonomy — how independent the company is from external financiers, (2) accountability — who has a legal claim if things go wrong and in what order, and (3) financial risk — how much fixed obligation (interest, principal) the company must service regardless of how business performs.

Debt is cheaper than equity (interest is tax-deductible and creditors take less risk than owners, so they demand a lower return) but it is a fixed, contractual obligation — missed interest or principal payments can trigger default regardless of how the year actually went. Equity is more expensive (owners bear residual risk and demand a higher return) but it is flexible — there is no contractual obligation to pay a dividend in a bad year.

This is the risk–return trade-off of financing: more debt lowers the average cost of capital (cheap debt pulls down the blended rate) and can amplify returns to equity holders in good years (financial leverage), but it raises the probability and severity of financial distress in bad years. More equity raises the average cost of capital but buys safety and independence.

RatioHigher value impliesLower value implies
Liabilities / Total CapitalGreater financial leverage — can lift ROE, but raises financial risk and debt-service burdenLower financial risk, but possibly under-using the tax shield and leverage benefits of debt
Equity / Total CapitalStrong financial autonomy and stability; less exposure to refinancing riskWeaker independence, more vulnerable to interest-rate and refinancing risk
Retained Earnings / Total CapitalStrong internal financing capacity — growth doesn't depend on outside capitalMay indicate recurring losses or heavy payouts limiting reinvestment

Capital structure is a statement about who bears the risk of the business not working out as planned. A heavily-equity-financed firm is more resilient to shocks but may be forgoing the return-amplifying power of leverage; a heavily-debt-financed firm can grow faster and reward equity holders more, but a single bad year can threaten its survival.

TAN's equity ratio rose from 92.24% (2022) to 96.60% (2023) — an Equity Multiplier (Total Assets / Total Equity) falling from 1.08 to 1.04. TAN is financed almost entirely by its owners, with virtually no leverage. This is extremely safe, but it also means TAN is not using debt's tax shield or leverage to boost returns to shareholders — a textbook talking point for the risk-return trade-off.

  • Stating "more equity is always better" — it is safer, not strictly better; it also raises the cost of capital and can dilute returns.
  • Confusing financial risk (from capital structure / leverage) with business risk (from the nature of operations) — they are different, additive sources of total risk.
  • Forgetting that the cost of debt is usually lower than the cost of equity, which is why moderate leverage can be efficient, not just risky.
🏦 More Debt

Pros: cheaper cost of capital, interest tax shield, can amplify ROE through leverage, no ownership dilution.

Cons: fixed obligation regardless of performance, higher financial risk, possible covenants restricting management, refinancing risk.

🧾 More Equity

Pros: no obligatory repayment, greater financial autonomy and stability, more resilience to downturns.

Cons: higher cost of capital, ownership dilution for new shareholders, may under-utilise leverage's return-boosting effect.

The core trade-off, in one sentence

Financial autonomy and financial risk move in opposite directions: the more a company finances itself with equity, the more independent and stable it is, but the more it gives up the cheaper cost and leverage benefits that debt provides — and vice versa.

Exam Prep

Essay questions on Capital Analysis

“Discuss the trade-off between debt and equity financing. Why might two equally profitable companies choose very different capital structures?
Model answer outline

Open with the cost-vs-risk framing: debt is cheaper (tax-deductible interest, lower required return for lower-risk creditors) but contractually fixed; equity is more expensive but flexible and loss-absorbing.

Explain financial leverage: moderate debt can raise ROE when operating returns exceed the cost of debt, but it raises the variance of returns to equity holders and the probability of distress.

Address the "two equally profitable firms" prompt directly: differences can come from industry volatility (a stable-cash-flow utility can safely carry more debt than a cyclical manufacturer), management risk appetite, access to capital markets, growth-stage (young firms often favour equity to preserve flexibility), and existing covenant constraints.

Close by stating there is no single optimal structure — only one that is appropriate to the company's risk profile, growth needs, and cash-flow stability.

“A company's equity ratio rose from 70% to 95% over three years entirely because it repaid all its debt. Is this necessarily a sign of improving financial health?
Model answer outline

Acknowledge the surface positive: lower financial risk, stronger autonomy, no debt-service burden.

Then complicate it (this is what earns marks): a 95% equity ratio may also indicate the company is not using leverage efficiently — forgoing cheap financing and the tax shield, potentially leaving growth opportunities unfunded, and possibly producing a lower ROE than a moderately-levered peer with the same operating performance. Financial health requires assessing this alongside profitability and growth, not capital structure in isolation.

financial autonomy financial leverage cost of capital tax shield equity dilution financial risk vs business risk internal vs external financing
§ 4.3 — Connecting the Two Sides

Analysis of Sources and Uses of Funds

Asset analysis and capital analysis each look at one side of the balance sheet in isolation. Sources-and-uses analysis is where you put them back together and trace the flow of money between them during the period — literally double-entry bookkeeping logic turned into a diagnostic tool.

A statement that reclassifies every balance-sheet change between two dates into one of two buckets: a Use of Funds (money was deployed) or a Source of Funds (money was raised/released). Because the balance sheet must balance, total uses always equal total sources — a useful self-check on your own workings.

It is the single best tool for answering "what did this company actually do financially this year?" in one page — did it raise new debt to fund expansion? Did it sell off inventory to pay down loans? Did profit alone fund growth? Lenders use it to judge whether financing requests are being used as proposed; managers use it to audit their own capital allocation.

This is the logic students most often memorise without understanding — so reason it through instead:

  • Assets are where cash goes when it is spent or deployed. Buying more inventory, letting receivables grow, purchasing equipment — every one of these consumes cash. So an asset increase is a use of funds. Conversely, an asset decrease (selling inventory, collecting receivables, selling equipment) releases cash back to the company — a source of funds.
  • Liabilities and equity are where cash comes from. Borrowing more, or issuing more shares, brings cash into the company — a source of funds. Repaying a loan or buying back shares sends cash out — a use of funds.

The whole table is really just the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) read as a story of cash flowing in and out.

Look at the composition of sources, not just the total. Funding growth mainly from operating cash flow / retained earnings is the highest-quality source. Funding growth mainly from new short-term debt is the lowest-quality and riskiest. On the uses side, check whether spending matched stated strategy (e.g. genuine capex for expansion vs. funds quietly absorbed by a receivables build-up).

  • Flipping the rule for liabilities/equity — remember it is the opposite direction from assets (increase = source, not use).
  • Forgetting that total uses must equal total sources; if your table doesn't balance, you have a classification error.
  • Stopping at the table without Step 4's interpretation — examiners want the "so what", not just the arithmetic.
The classification rule
↑ Assets → USE of Funds  |  ↓ Assets → SOURCE of Funds
↑ Liabilities or Equity → SOURCE of Funds  |  ↓ Liabilities or Equity → USE of Funds
Method

The four-step procedure

① Condense the balance sheet

Group minor or similar-natured items so the statement highlights what matters for this analysis' purpose, instead of drowning in line-items.

② Build the statement of changes

List every item's change between two balance-sheet dates and sort it into the Use / Source column using the rule above.

③ Summarise into the uses/sources table

Aggregate into Increase Assets / Decrease Liabilities / Decrease Equity (Uses) versus Decrease Assets / Increase Liabilities / Increase Equity (Sources), each as value and %.

④ Interpret

Answer three questions: what was the primary capital need? what were the main sources, and their character? do sources and uses match appropriately in time-horizon and risk?

Exam Prep

Essay questions on Sources & Uses of Funds

“Explain why an increase in accounts receivable is classified as a use of funds while an increase in accounts payable is classified as a source of funds.
Model answer outline

Receivables are an asset representing cash the company is owed but has not yet collected — letting this balance grow means the company is effectively financing its customers, tying up cash that would otherwise be available, hence a use of funds. Payables are a liability representing cash the company owes but has not yet paid — letting this balance grow means suppliers are effectively financing the company, freeing up cash for other purposes, hence a source of funds. Both illustrate the same underlying idea: an asset increase consumes cash; a liability increase provides cash.

§ 4.4 — The Time-Matching Principle

Financial Balance Analysis & Net Working Capital

This is the conceptual heart of the chapter. Every other tool in §4.1–4.3 describes what a company holds and how it's financed. Financial balance analysis asks the question that actually determines survival: is the timing of financing matched to the timing of the assets it funds?

NWC is the cushion of long-term capital left over after funding all long-term assets — equivalently, the portion of current assets financed by long-term (stable) capital rather than short-term capital.

This is the golden rule of financing: the maturity of a financing source should match the time horizon of what it funds. A factory takes years to generate the cash flow that repays its cost — it should be financed with capital that won't be called back for years (long-term debt, equity). If a factory is financed with a 90-day loan, the company will need to refinance that loan again and again for the asset's entire life, and any single failed refinancing (a credit crunch, a bank's change of heart) can force a fire-sale of a productive asset just to survive. Short-term assets like inventory, by contrast, naturally convert to cash quickly and so can safely be financed with short-term capital.

NWC is the single number creditors and analysts check first when assessing structural (as opposed to day-to-day) liquidity risk. A company can be profitable and still collapse if its financial structure is mismatched — this is why "profitable companies going bankrupt" is a real and recurring phenomenon, almost always traceable to a financing mismatch like negative NWC combined with a sudden inability to roll over short-term debt.

Deriving the formula — two equivalent views of the same number
NWC = Long-term Capital (LTC) − Long-term Assets (LTA)  // the financing view
NWC = Current Assets (CA) − Current Liabilities (CL)  // the liquidity view
(They're identical because Assets ≡ Capital: LTA+CA = LTC+CL, so LTC−LTA = CA−CL, always.)
Why two formulas for one concept?

The first formula tells you where the surplus came from (financing decisions). The second tells you what it's available for (covering current assets / day-to-day liquidity). A strong essay answer uses whichever framing fits the question — and ideally shows you know they're the same number viewed from two sides of the balance sheet.

Case 1 · NWC > 0

LTC > LTA. All long-term assets are covered by long-term capital, with a surplus left over to help fund current assets.

Advantage: structurally safe, low refinancing risk.
Disadvantage: if the surplus is excessive, long-term (more expensive) capital is being used to fund assets that didn't need it — raising the cost of capital unnecessarily.

Case 2 · NWC = 0

LTC = LTA exactly. Long-term capital covers long-term assets with nothing left over; current assets are funded entirely by current liabilities.

Structurally "balanced" on paper, but fragile: there is zero cushion. Any disruption to short-term financing (a supplier tightening terms, a bank recalling a credit line) leaves current assets under-funded immediately.

Case 3 · NWC < 0

LTC < LTA. Long-term capital cannot even cover long-term assets — part of the company's fixed, illiquid asset base is being financed with short-term capital.

Why this is dangerous: short-term capital must be renewed every few months, but the long-term asset it's funding won't generate cash for years. The company is structurally dependent on continuously refinancing short-term debt just to keep operating — a single refusal to roll it over can force distress.

4.4.2

Quantifying "how reasonable" or "how severe" the structure is

Knowing the sign of NWC isn't the end of the analysis — within Case 1, a company can still be financed inefficiently; within Case 3, the degree of imbalance matters enormously.

If NWC > 0 — how is current-asset financing split?
LT capital financing CA % = (LTC − LTA) / CA = NWC / CA
ST capital financing CA % = CL / CA

The higher the long-term share, the lower the refinancing risk — but the higher the average cost of capital, since long-term financing is generally pricier than short-term.

If NWC < 0 — how severe is the shortfall?
ST capital financing LTA % = (LTA − LTC) / LTA = −NWC / LTA

This is the share of fixed/long-term assets propped up by capital that could be recalled within a year. The higher this percentage, the more severe and urgent the financing mismatch.

Exam Prep

Essay questions on Financial Balance Analysis

“"A company can be profitable yet still face the risk of bankruptcy." Use the concept of Net Working Capital to explain how this is possible.
Model answer outline

Open by separating two distinct dimensions of financial health: profitability (income-statement performance) and financial structure / balance (balance-sheet time-matching). They are not the same thing.

Explain the mechanism: if NWC < 0, part of the company's long-term assets is financed by short-term capital that must be continually renewed. Even a highly profitable company generates most of its cash slowly, over the life of its fixed assets — it cannot suddenly produce enough cash to repay a short-term loan on demand. If a lender refuses to roll over that short-term financing (due to a credit crunch, a change in risk appetite, or a covenant breach unrelated to profitability), the company faces a genuine liquidity crisis despite healthy operating profit.

Conclude: this is precisely why financial balance analysis is treated as a structural risk indicator independent of profitability — a firm must be analysed on both dimensions, not profitability alone.

“Is NWC = 0 a healthy financial position? Explain why this case is often described as "balanced but fragile."
Model answer outline

Yes, technically balanced — long-term capital exactly covers long-term assets, satisfying the basic time-matching rule, so it is not classified as imbalanced. However, it provides zero margin of safety: 100% of current assets depend on current liabilities being continuously available. Any shock — a supplier shortening payment terms, a bank reducing a credit line, a slow sales month delaying collections — immediately threatens the company's ability to fund its current assets, with no cushion to absorb it. "Fragile" captures that the structure works only as long as nothing goes wrong.
