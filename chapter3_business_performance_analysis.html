Why this chapter exists

A single year's income statement tells you what a company earned. It can't tell you whether that's good, improving, or about to fall apart. Chapter 3 gives you the two simplest, most exam-relevant tools for adding that missing dimension — time and proportion — to the income statement.

3.1 in one line

Horizontal analysis

Lines up the same item across different periods and asks: how much did it move, in money and in percent?

3.2 in one line

Vertical analysis

Lines up every item within one period against net revenue and asks: what share of every revenue dong does this item eat or keep?

Exam format reminder
Your end-of-chapter assessment is a short-essay (tự luận) paper. Examiners are not just checking whether you can compute a percentage — they're checking whether you can state the formula, compute it correctly, read the direction and size of the change, and explain what it implies about the business. Every section below is built around that four-step habit.

3.1 — Horizontal Analysis

Purpose

To observe how financial indicators trend over time — the amount and percentage by which each item rose or fell — so you can judge the company's growth momentum and where it sits in its development cycle.

Method

The horizontal comparative method: place the same line item from consecutive (or otherwise related) periods side by side.

What gets compared

Income-statement items are compared across periods — monthly, quarterly, or annually — by looking at the change between the start and end of a period, or across several time points in sequence. The base period you compare against can be:

historical data (last year) planned / budgeted targets industry averages

The two-step process

  1. Calculate the change. For every item (revenue, expenses, profit), find both the absolute change and the relative (%) change between the analysis period and the base period.
  2. Interpret the trend. Read what the direction and size of each change says about the business, then propose appropriate explanations or recommendations — this is the step essay questions actually grade you on.
Step 1 — Formulas Absolute change  =  Itemanalysis period  −  Itembase period
Relative change  =  ( Absolute change  ÷  Itembase period )  × 100%
Common slip
The denominator is always the base period value — never the analysis-period value, and never net revenue. Horizontal analysis is item-against-itself, across time. (That's vertical analysis's job — see 3.2.)

3.2 — Vertical Analysis

Purpose

To judge cost efficiency and see how each profit component contributes to the company's net profit margin — by expressing every line as a share of one common base, within a single period.

Method

The vertical comparison method: every item is divided by the same base figure, then those ratios are compared across periods (month, quarter, half-year, year) or against each other.

Choosing the base — it depends on the statement

Income statement
Net Revenue = 100%
Balance sheet
Total Assets = 100%
Cash flow statement
Net Cash Flow = 100%

This chapter focuses on the income statement, so Net Revenue is your base unless a question says otherwise. Comparison data can again be historical, planned, or industry-average figures.

The three-step process

  1. Rearrange the income statement by profit component (gross profit, operating profit, profit before tax, net profit after tax) so each layer is visible.
  2. Divide every item by net revenue to get its percentage share.
  3. Analyse the trend of those ratios across periods and propose recommendations.
Core formula Item as % of revenue  =  ( Item ÷ Net Revenue )  × 100%
Horizontal vs. vertical, in one sentence
Horizontal asks "how did this item change against itself over time?" Vertical asks "how big is this item next to revenue, right now — and is that share growing or shrinking?" A complete essay answer almost always uses both.

How to narrate this table in an essay

"Net revenue fell 8.4% in 2023 before rebounding 27.3% in 2024, but gross profit grew in both years (+41.2%, then +34.3%) — meaning HP protected its margin even while revenue was shrinking. The vertical view confirms this: COGS fell from 91.8% to 86.7% of revenue across the period, so gross margin expanded from 8.2% to 13.3%. Notice the shape: state the number → name the direction → connect two metrics → flag a caveat. That's a complete short-essay paragraph.

3.4 — Reading what moved ROS

Your slides ask questions like "How did selling expenses affect ROS in 2023 vs 2022?" This is the single most common essay question type for this chapter, so it deserves its own walkthrough.

Return on Sales (net profit margin) ROS  =  Net Profit After Tax (EAT) ÷ Net Revenue  × 100%

Because vertical analysis already expresses every item — including EAT — as a % of net revenue, the EAT row of your vertical table is ROS. That means the point-change of any other row tells you exactly how much that item pulled ROS up or down.

The one rule to memorise
If a line is an expense, a rising % of revenue drags ROS down by that many points; a falling % lifts ROS up by that many points. If a line is income, it works the other way round.

3.5 — Essay Question Bank & Self-Check Quiz

Sentence starters for the written exam

Horizontal-analysis sentence

"[Item] [increased/decreased] by VND [X] million ([Y]%) in [year] compared with [base year], mainly driven by ___."

Vertical-analysis sentence

"[Item] accounted for [X]% of net revenue in [year], [up/down] from [Y]% in [base year], which indicates ___."

ROS-bridge sentence

"ROS moved by [X] percentage points between [year] and [year]; [item]'s ratio change of [Y] points was the [largest/a minor] contributor because ___."

Caveat sentence (use when % looks extreme)

"Although the percentage change looks large ([Y]%), the absolute amount (VND [X] million) is modest because the base-period figure was small."
