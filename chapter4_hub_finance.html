Why must a balance sheet balance — and what happens when it doesn't?

Chapter 4 is one question asked five ways: is the money a company raised matched, in time and in risk, to how it is being spent? Master that and NWC, WCR, asset structure, capital structure all become logical — not arbitrary.

§ 4.1 — The Asset Side

Asset Analysis: Where Did the Money Go?

Every dollar on the asset side represents a decision already made. Asset analysis reads those decisions back out of the numbers and judges whether they were wise.

4.1.1

Asset Fluctuation Analysis (Horizontal)

AWhat is it?

Horizontal analysis tracks one asset item across time — month-to-month, year-to-year — computing absolute change (e.g. +35,000 million VND) and relative change (e.g. +8.4%). It answers how is this number moving, not how big is it now.

BWhy does it matter?
  • Managers use it to check whether investment decisions are happening as planned and at the right pace.
  • Creditors watch whether the assets backing their loan are growing, shrinking, or converting into something less liquid.
  • Investors read fluctuation as a proxy for strategy in motion — expansion, contraction, or a pivot in business model.
CWhat is the financial logic?

An asset cannot grow on its own — every increase must be financed by something. This is the seed of §4.3: asset fluctuation is "uses of funds" viewed item-by-item. Before judging an increase, always ask: what financed it, and is that financing appropriate for this asset's time horizon?

DHow to interpret increases & decreases
Item↑ Increase usually means↓ Decrease usually means
Cash & equivalentsLiquidity improves — but idle cash earns littleTighter liquidity, or cash put to productive use
Accounts receivableLooser credit terms — capital tied up, bad-debt risk risesFaster collection / stricter policy — good for liquidity
InventoriesExpecting demand growth — or early overstockingLeaner operations, or risk of stock-outs
Fixed assetsCapacity expansion, higher operating leverageDivestment, depreciation outpacing capex
GCommon student mistakes
  • Reporting only % change and ignoring absolute amount — 783% on a tiny base may be financially trivial.
  • Calling every increase "growth" without asking which item moved and why.
  • Forgetting to connect asset fluctuation to its financing source (the §4.3 link).

4.1.2

Asset Structure Analysis (Vertical)

AWhat is it?

Vertical analysis expresses every asset as a % of total assets. It answers "how is the pie sliced right now — and is the company over-concentrated in one slice?"

CFinancial logic: asset structure = business-model fingerprint

A trading company naturally carries high inventory and receivables with low fixed assets. A manufacturer or utility carries heavy fixed assets. A bank carries almost no inventory. There is no universally "correct" structure — only one that is, or isn't, consistent with how the company earns revenue. Use industry norms as a benchmark.

EImplications of asset structure decisions
⚖ Current vs Non-current

Current = liquidity reserve; non-current = productive capacity. The balance is a trade-off between flexibility and scale of operation.

🏭 Rising Fixed Assets

Signals capacity expansion. Raises operating leverage: fixed costs (depreciation) stay constant regardless of sales — profit becomes more volatile.

📦 Rising Inventories

Can mean positioning for demand growth or that goods aren't selling. Check whether inventory growth is proportional to — or faster than — revenue.

Exam

Essay questions — Asset Analysis

"Explain asset structure analysis. What might a rising share of fixed assets in total assets indicate about a company's strategy and risk?
Model answer outline

Define: Vertical analysis expresses each asset as % of total assets to assess diversification and risk exposure; combined with horizontal analysis to track how that structure evolves.

Rising fixed assets: Signals capacity-expansion strategy — investment in production lines or equipment to support anticipated demand. Raises operating leverage: higher fixed costs (depreciation) make profit more sensitive to revenue swings.

Risk: Ask whether the fixed-asset growth was financed with long-term capital (appropriate per §4.4) or short-term capital (financing mismatch risk). Reasonable if demand forecasts hold and financing matches; otherwise raises both operating risk and financial risk simultaneously.

"A company's inventories increased 30% while sales grew only 8%. Is this positive or negative? Justify your answer.
Model answer outline

Lead with the mismatch: inventory growth (30%) outpacing sales (8%) is a warning sign, not automatically negative. Two interpretations: (1) deliberate stock-build ahead of expected demand — defensible if forward orders support it; (2) slowing sell-through / overstocking — inefficient capital use and rising obsolescence risk. A strong answer evaluates both and states what further evidence (inventory turnover, order backlog) would resolve the ambiguity rather than picking one side without justification.

§ 4.2 — The Capital Side

Capital Analysis: Whose Money Is It, and What Does That Cost?

Capital analysis asks the mirror question: where did the money come from, and what claim does that source hold over the company? Every source — supplier credit, bank loan, shareholder investment — carries a different price, deadline, and amount of control surrendered.

4.2.1

Capital Fluctuation Analysis (Horizontal)

AWhat is it?

The same horizontal logic as §4.1.1, applied to liabilities and equity: track each capital item's absolute and % change across periods to assess the company's capacity to mobilise financial resources.

DHow to interpret changes in capital items
Item↑ Increase usually means↓ Decrease usually means
Accounts payableGreater reliance on supplier credit — efficient, or a sign of cash strainPaying faster — builds supplier trust, but uses cash sooner
Short-term borrowingsFinancing a short-term need or covering a liquidity gapDeleveraging — improves safety, frees future cash flow
Retained earningsCompany profitable and reinvesting — stronger internal financingLosses or heavy dividends — weakens reinvestment capacity
Shareholders' equityNew shares issued — permanent capital, but ownership dilutedShare buybacks or losses — may signal confidence or distress

4.2.2

Capital Structure Analysis (Vertical) — the Debt vs. Equity Trade-off

CFinancial logic: risk, autonomy & cost of capital

Debt is cheaper than equity (interest is tax-deductible; creditors take less risk so they demand a lower return) but it creates a fixed, contractual obligation — missed payments can trigger default regardless of how the year went.

Equity is more expensive (owners bear residual risk) but flexible — no contractual obligation to pay a dividend in a bad year.

This is the risk–return trade-off of financing: more debt lowers average cost of capital and can amplify ROE in good years (financial leverage), but raises probability and severity of distress in bad years. More equity raises cost of capital but buys safety and independence.

DHow to interpret capital structure ratios
RatioHigher value impliesLower value implies
Liabilities / Total CapitalGreater financial leverage — can lift ROE, but raises financial risk and debt-service burdenLower financial risk; possibly under-using debt's tax shield
Equity / Total CapitalStrong financial autonomy and stability; less refinancing riskWeaker independence; more vulnerable to credit-market changes
Retained Earnings / Total CapitalStrong internal financing — growth doesn't depend on outside capitalRecurring losses or heavy payouts limiting reinvestment
GCommon student mistakes
  • "More equity is always better" — it is safer, not strictly better; it raises cost of capital and can reduce returns.
  • Confusing financial risk (from leverage) with business/operating risk (from operations) — they are different, additive sources of total risk.
  • Forgetting that the cost of debt is usually lower than the cost of equity — moderate leverage can be efficient, not just risky.
🏦 More Debt — trade-offs

Pros: cheaper cost of capital, interest tax shield, amplifies ROE, no ownership dilution.

Cons: fixed obligation regardless of performance, higher financial risk, refinancing risk, possible restrictive covenants.

🧾 More Equity — trade-offs

Pros: no obligatory repayment, greater financial autonomy, more resilience to downturns, no covenants.

Cons: higher cost of capital, ownership dilution, may under-utilise leverage's return-boosting effect.

Core trade-off in one sentence

Financial autonomy and financial risk move in opposite directions. The more a company finances itself with equity, the more independent and stable it is — but the more it gives up debt's cheaper cost and leverage benefits. And vice versa.

Exam

Essay questions — Capital Analysis

"Discuss the trade-off between debt and equity financing. Why might two equally profitable companies choose very different capital structures?
Model answer outline

Frame the trade-off: Debt is cheaper (tax-deductible interest, lower required return) but contractually fixed; equity is more expensive but flexible and loss-absorbing.

Financial leverage: Moderate debt raises ROE when operating returns exceed cost of debt, but increases variance and probability of distress.

Two equally profitable firms differ because of: industry volatility (stable utility can safely carry more debt than cyclical manufacturer), management risk appetite, access to capital markets, growth stage, and existing covenant constraints.

Conclusion: No single optimal structure exists — only one appropriate to the company's risk profile, growth needs, and cash-flow stability.

§ 4.3 — Connecting Both Sides

Analysis of Sources and Uses of Funds

§4.1 and §4.2 look at each side in isolation. Sources-and-uses analysis puts them back together and traces the flow of money — double-entry bookkeeping logic turned into a diagnostic tool.

AWhat is it?

A statement that reclassifies every balance-sheet change between two dates into a Use of Funds (money was deployed) or a Source of Funds (money was raised/released). Because the balance sheet must balance, total uses always equal total sources — a useful self-check.

CWhy are asset increases a "use" and liability/equity increases a "source"?

Reason it through — don't memorise:

  • Assets are where cash goes when deployed. Buying inventory, letting receivables grow, purchasing equipment — every one consumes cash → use of funds. Conversely, an asset decrease releases cash → source of funds.
  • Liabilities and equity are where cash comes from. Borrowing more or issuing shares brings cash in → source of funds. Repaying a loan sends cash out → use of funds.
GCommon student mistakes
  • Flipping the rule for liabilities/equity — it is the opposite direction from assets.
  • Forgetting that total uses must equal total sources; an unbalanced table means a classification error.
  • Stopping at the table without Step 4's interpretation — examiners want the "so what", not just the arithmetic.
Classification rule — the whole framework in two lines
↑ Assets → USE of Funds  |  ↓ Assets → SOURCE of Funds
↑ Liabilities or Equity → SOURCE of Funds  |  ↓ Liabilities or Equity → USE of Funds
Self-check: Total Uses = Total Sources always
Method

The four-step procedure

① Condense the balance sheet

Group minor or similar items so the statement highlights what matters, not drowning in line items. The level of detail depends on the purpose of the analysis.

② Build the statement of changes

List every item's change between two dates and classify each as Use or Source using the rule above. Every line must go into exactly one column.

③ Summarise into Uses / Sources table

Aggregate: (↑Assets + ↓Liabilities + ↓Equity) = Total Uses. (↓Assets + ↑Liabilities + ↑Equity) = Total Sources. Compute each as value and %.

④ Interpret (the critical step)

Answer three questions: What was the primary capital need? What were the main sources and their character? Do sources and uses match in time-horizon and risk?

Exam

Essay questions — Sources & Uses

"Why is an increase in accounts receivable a use of funds, while an increase in accounts payable is a source of funds?
Model answer outline

Receivables are an asset representing cash owed but not yet collected — letting this balance grow means the company is financing its customers, tying up cash: a use of funds. Payables are a liability representing cash owed but not yet paid — letting this balance grow means suppliers are financing the company, freeing up cash: a source of funds. Both illustrate the same underlying idea: an asset increase consumes cash; a liability increase provides cash.

§ 4.4 — The Time-Matching Principle

Financial Balance Analysis & Net Working Capital (NWC)

This is the conceptual heart of the chapter. Financial balance analysis asks the question that determines survival: is the timing of financing matched to the timing of the assets it funds?

AWhat is Net Working Capital (NWC)?

NWC is the cushion of long-term capital left over after funding all long-term assets — equivalently, the portion of current assets financed by long-term (stable) capital rather than short-term capital. It is the primary indicator of structural financial balance.

CThe golden rule of financing — why must maturity match?

The golden rule of financing: the maturity of a financing source should match the time horizon of what it funds. A factory takes years to generate cash flow — finance it with capital that won't be recalled for years (long-term debt, equity). If financed with a 90-day loan, the company must refinance repeatedly, and any single failed refinancing can force a fire-sale of a productive asset. Short-term assets like inventory naturally convert to cash quickly and can safely be financed with short-term capital.

BWhy does NWC matter — who uses it?

NWC is the first number creditors and analysts check when assessing structural (not just day-to-day) liquidity risk. A company can be profitable and still collapse if its financial structure is mismatched — this is why "profitable companies going bankrupt" is a real and recurring phenomenon, almost always traceable to a negative-NWC financing mismatch.

NWC — two equivalent views of the same number
NWC = Long-term Capital (LTC) − Long-term Assets (LTA)  ← financing view
NWC = Current Assets (CA) − Current Liabilities (CL)  ← liquidity view
Proof: LTA + CA = LTC + CL (balance-sheet identity), so LTC − LTA = CA − CL ✓ They are always identical.
Why two formulas?

The first tells you where the surplus came from (financing decisions). The second tells you what it's available for (day-to-day liquidity). A strong essay uses whichever framing fits the question — and shows you know they are the same number viewed from both sides of the balance sheet.

Case 1 · NWC > 0 ✓

LTC > LTA. All long-term assets covered by long-term capital, with a surplus helping fund current assets. Structurally safe, low refinancing risk.

Watch for: if surplus is excessive, expensive long-term capital is funding assets that don't need it — unnecessarily raising cost of capital.

Case 2 · NWC = 0 ⚠

LTC = LTA exactly. Current assets funded entirely by current liabilities — zero cushion.

Structurally "balanced" on paper but fragile: any disruption to short-term financing immediately leaves current assets under-funded. Works only if nothing goes wrong.

Case 3 · NWC < 0 ✗

LTC < LTA. Long-term capital cannot cover long-term assets — part of the fixed asset base is financed with short-term capital.

Dangerous: short-term capital must be renewed periodically, but the asset won't generate cash for years. A single refusal to roll over can force financial distress.

4.4.2

Quantifying: how safe or how severe?

If NWC > 0 — how is current-asset financing split?
LT capital financing CA% = (LTC − LTA) / CA
ST capital financing CA% = CL / CA

Higher long-term share = lower refinancing risk, but higher cost of capital.

If NWC < 0 — how severe is the shortfall?
ST capital financing LTA% = (LTA − LTC) / LTA

Higher % = more severe mismatch. This share of fixed assets could be recalled within a year — quantified distress risk.

Exam

Essay questions — Financial Balance

""A company can be profitable yet still face the risk of bankruptcy." Use the concept of Net Working Capital to explain how this is possible.
Model answer outline

Separate two dimensions: Profitability (income-statement performance) and financial balance (balance-sheet time-matching) are not the same. A company can score well on one and fail on the other.

Mechanism: If NWC < 0, part of long-term assets is financed by short-term capital that must be continually renewed. Even a highly profitable company generates cash slowly, over the life of its fixed assets — it cannot suddenly produce enough cash to repay a short-term loan on demand. If a lender refuses to roll over that financing (credit crunch, covenant breach), the company faces genuine liquidity crisis despite healthy profit.

Conclusion: A firm must be analysed on both profitability and financial balance — not profitability alone. This is why NWC is a structural risk indicator, not a performance indicator.

"Is NWC = 0 a healthy financial position? Why is it described as "balanced but fragile"?
Model answer outline

Yes, technically balanced — long-term capital exactly covers long-term assets, satisfying the basic time-matching rule. However, it provides zero margin of safety: 100% of current assets depend on current liabilities being continuously available. Any shock — a supplier shortening payment terms, a bank reducing a credit line, a slow sales month — immediately threatens the company's ability to fund its current assets. "Fragile" captures that the structure works only if nothing goes wrong.

§ 4.5 — The Operating Cycle

Working Capital Requirement (WCR)

NWC tells you whether the financial structure is safe. WCR tells you how much working capital the company operationally needs just to keep day-to-day business running.

AWhat is WCR?

Working Capital Requirement (WCR) is the amount of capital required to finance the gap between when money goes out and when money comes back in from operating activities.

WCR = Current Assets (CA) − Short-term Operating Liabilities (STOL)
STOL = current liabilities from operations (payables, accruals) — NOT short-term bank debt
CWhy does WCR exist? The cash-cycle logic

WCR arises from a timing mismatch in operations:

  • You pay suppliers for raw materials now (or in 30 days)
  • You hold inventory for weeks or months while processing and selling
  • You sell on credit and wait 60+ days to collect cash

The gap between paying out and collecting back in must be financed by working capital. Longer cycle = more WCR needed. Shorter cycle = less.

Pay Suppliers ──── Hold Inventory ──── Sell on Credit ──── Collect Cash
←————— THIS ENTIRE GAP MUST BE FINANCED BY WCR —————→
DHow to interpret WCR changes
WCR changeDriven byImplication
WCR ↑Receivables growing, inventories buildingCompany needs MORE cash to fund operations — monitor liquidity
WCR ↓Faster collections, inventory reduction, extended payablesOperations more cash-efficient — positive if driven by improved terms
WCR < 0Suppliers finance the company (STOL > CA)Very cash-efficient — common in retail. Sustainable only with strong bargaining power
EHow can managers reduce WCR?
  • Reduce receivables: tighter credit policies, faster collection, invoice factoring
  • Reduce inventory: just-in-time procurement, better demand forecasting, faster production cycles
  • Extend payables: negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers (without damaging relationships)

The growth trap: Growing companies often need more WCR even as they become more profitable — sales growth requires proportionally more inventory and receivables. Fast-growing profitable companies can still run out of cash because profit grows slower than WCR.

GCommon student mistakes — especially NWC vs WCR
  • Confusing WCR with NWC — NWC is about financial structure (is long-term financing adequate?); WCR is about operational cash needs (how much does the operating cycle consume?).
  • Including short-term bank debt in STOL — it is a financing choice, not an automatic operating consequence. Only trade payables, accruals, and customer advances belong in STOL.
  • Treating any WCR increase as bad — for a growing company, rising WCR can simply reflect healthy business expansion.

Exam

Essay questions — WCR

"Explain why a rapidly growing, profitable company may face a cash shortage. Use the concept of Working Capital Requirement.
Model answer outline

Define WCR: Capital required to bridge the gap between cash going out (paying suppliers, holding inventory) and cash coming back in (collecting from customers) — the financial cost of the operating cycle.

Link to growth: As revenue grows, a company must hold more inventory and carry more receivables. These increase CA faster than STOL typically grows, so WCR rises proportionally with revenue — often faster.

The growth trap: A profitable company generating 500m VND in net profit may simultaneously need 800m VND in additional working capital to support its growing sales — a net cash drain of 300m VND despite strong profitability. If not financed (through credit lines, equity, or more efficient cash conversion), the company can become insolvent while appearing profitable on paper.
