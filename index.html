1.1 Concept & Roles

Why financial analysis exists, and who actually reads the output.

Definition

What is corporate financial analysis?

Corporate financial analysis is the process of examining a firm's financial data in order to (1) evaluate performance, (2) detect potential opportunities and risks, and then (3) make decisions to achieve set objectives.

Essay tipNotice the definition has three moving parts — examine, evaluate/detect, decide. If an exam question asks you to "define and explain the purpose," structure your paragraph around exactly these three verbs instead of just repeating the sentence.

Inside 1.3.5

Audit opinions on the financial statements

Before trusting a Balance Sheet, Income Statement, or Cash Flow Statement, an analyst checks what the auditor said about it. There are four possible opinions:

✅ Unqualified — clean opinion
Statements are fairly presented, follow accounting rules, free of material errors. The best possible result.
⚠️ Qualified — fine, except for one issue
Fairly stated except for the possible effects of a misstatement that is material but not pervasive (i.e. isolated).
❌ Adverse — don't trust it
Errors are serious and widespread (pervasive). The statements do not give a true and fair view.
❓ Disclaimer — can't say anything
Auditor could not obtain enough evidence to form any conclusion. This is about uncertainty, not proven error.
The key distinctionQualified vs. Adverse is about scope — is the problem isolated (qualified) or does it taint the whole report (adverse)? Disclaimer is different in kind — the auditor never even got enough evidence to judge.

1.5 Methods of Corporate Financial Analysis

Four methods. Type your own figures into each worksheet below the explanation — watch the analyst's conclusion stamp itself in real time.

1.5.1

Comparative Method

Compares quantifiable indicators of the same content and similar characteristics to find a trend — up, down, or stable.

Conditions for comparability: (1) uniform economic content & calculation method, (2) same time length / corresponding points in time, (3) same unit of measurement.

Horizontal comparison

Compares the ending number vs. the beginning number of one item, in both absolute and relative (%) terms — reveals how that single item moved over time.

Absolute change = Item(Analysis Year) − Item(Base Year)
Relative change = Absolute change ÷ Item(Base Year) × 100%

Vertical comparison

Compares the weight of one line item against a base total (Net Revenue for the Income Statement; Total Assets for the Balance Sheet) — reveals structure, and lets you compare firms of different sizes.

Percentage = Line Item Amount ÷ Base Amount × 100%

Pros & cons

✓ Pros

Tracks trends over time · visualizes growth/seasonality · aids forecasting

✗ Cons

Ignores detail inside one period · distorted by one-off events · doesn't explain why the change happened

1.5.2

Ratio Analysis

Describes the significant relationship between different line items, to assess profitability, solvency, and efficiency.

Activity Ratios
Efficiency of operations (e.g. inventory turnover).
Liquidity Ratios
Ability to meet short-term obligations.
Solvency Ratios
Ability to meet long-term obligations.
Profitability Ratios
Ability to generate profit from assets or sales.

5-step process: Select relevant ratios → Calculate them → Compare with benchmarks (prior periods / industry / targets) → Interpret the results → Draw conclusions & decide.

1.5.3

Breakdown (Decomposition) Method

Deconstructs one complex indicator into its underlying components, so you can see what caused the change.

4 steps: Break down the indicator → analyze how each component moves over time → assess each component's contribution → summarize the main driver(s) of the overall change.

ROA = EAT ÷ Ābq = (EAT ÷ S) × (S ÷ Ābq)
↳ Cost-saving efficiency × Asset efficiency

1.5.4

Graphical Method

Visually represents financial data so trends and anomalies jump out immediately.

📈 Line graphs

Best for change in one or few items over many periods — revenue, profit, or stock price trends.

📊 Bar charts

Best for comparing amounts across categories or periods — e.g. revenue by year or by business unit.

🥧 Pie charts

Best for showing the proportion of each part to a whole — e.g. cost structure, asset allocation.

📶 Stacked bar charts

Best when you need both the total and its composition at once — e.g. total liabilities split into short- and long-term.
