Chapter 5

Analysis of Corporate Cash Flow

Profit is an opinion. Cash is a fact. This chapter is about reading the statement of cash flows closely enough to tell which one a company is actually running on — and what that says about its liquidity, its investment direction, its financing habits, and how all three connect.

Opening case · Rite Aid

Profit looked survivable. The cash didn't.

Once the largest U.S. retail drugstore chain — and a cautionary tale in working capital management.

Starting in 2023, Rite Aid's financial trouble traced back to an aggressive expansion strategy: more than 1,600 new stores and $2.9 billion spent on acquisitions. That spending drained the company's cash reserves and quietly eroded profitability long before the headlines caught up. The company filed for bankruptcy in October 2023, and by May 2025 it was liquidating its remaining assets.

The takeaway for this chapter: the statement of cash flows can flag financial risk early — particularly when investing cash outflows are significantly larger than operating cash inflows. That single comparison (CFI vs. CFO) is one of the fastest red-flag checks an analyst has.

Learning outcomes

By the end of this chapter you should be able to

  • Read fluctuations in operating cash flow to judge debt-repayment capacity, profit-distribution capability, and room to expand investment.
  • Read changes in investing cash flow to identify a company's investment orientation.
  • Read changes in financing cash flow to evaluate how dependent a company is on external funding.
  • Explain why net operating cash flow and accounting profit diverge — and quantify that gap.
  • Analyze how the three cash flows interact to judge how well a company manages its financial resources overall.
The big picture

Three rivers, one pool

Every cash flow statement splits a company's cash movements into three activities. Click each river below.

Operating · NCFO Cash generated (or used) by the company's core, day-to-day business — sales collected, suppliers and staff paid, taxes and interest settled. This is the cash a business should ultimately be able to live on.
Why analyze cash flow at all

Four jobs this analysis does

  • Assess liquidity — can the company meet short-term obligations? Strong accounting profit can still hide a cash shortage; "profit is not cash."
  • Assess profit quality — how much of reported profit is backed by real, collectible cash rather than non-cash adjustments or one-off gains.
  • Support financial decisions — internal management uses this to decide on investment, dividends, and borrowing.
  • Understand interrelationships — how operating, investing, and financing activities lean on each other to keep the company funded.
§5.1

Analysis of Cash Flow from Operating Activities

This is the section examiners love to test, because it forces you to explain the single most important idea in the chapter: why operating cash flow and accounting profit are not the same number.

5.1.1 · Trend analysis of OCF

Method: horizontal (time-series) analysis

Purpose: examine how operating cash flow moves over time to judge whether the business is growing or shrinking.

Method: horizontal analysis — compare operating cash flow figures across periods (monthly, quarterly, yearly). The comparison base can be the company's own history, its plan/budget, or an industry average, depending on what question you're asking.

Data needed: operating cash flow figures from the cash flow statement, read alongside relevant macro- and micro-economic context for the period.

What you actually do: build a multi-year table of every line item that feeds operating cash flow, then calculate the absolute change and % change for each item to spot the trend and explain it.

5.1.2 · Why NOCF and EBT diverge

The bridge: EBT → NOCF (2024 case data)

Three blocks of adjustment carry you from accounting profit to operating cash: (1) non-cash items added back or removed (depreciation, provisions, FX, investment income, interest expense), (2) working-capital changes (receivables, inventory, payables, prepaid expenses), and (3) cash items EBT doesn't include yet (interest actually paid, tax actually paid, other operating cash items). §5.2

Analysis of Net Cash Flow Fluctuations

Same horizontal-analysis method as 5.1, but applied to the three net cash flows (NCFO, NCFI, NCFF) together — the goal is to read a company's financial life-stage and recent strategy from the shape of its cash flows.

Method

Purpose & method

Purpose: track how net cash flows change across periods (monthly, quarterly, yearly) to identify trends.

Method: horizontal comparison against historical data, planned/budgeted figures, or industry averages — whichever fits the question.

Reading it as a story: NCFO climbs every year and roughly doubles by 2024 — the core business is generating more cash. NCFI flips from positive in 2022 (likely recovering/divesting investments) to increasingly negative through 2024 — the company pivots toward expansion, funded internally. NCFF stays negative throughout but shrinks from a heavy −9,773 in 2022 to a lighter −2,198 in 2024 — large debt repayment early on, then far less reliance on external financing later. Put together, this looks like a maturing company self-funding growth from operations rather than from new borrowing.
§5.3

Analysis of Net Cash Flow Structure

Here the question changes from "how did it move" to "how much of the total does each activity make up." That's vertical analysis — and it has a sharp edge worth knowing for the exam.

Method

Purpose & method

Purpose: see how much each activity (operating / investing / financing) contributes to total net cash flow, to judge which activity drives the company's overall cash position.

Method: vertical analysis for the % structure in each period, combined with horizontal analysis to track how that structure shifts over time.

Worked example · same case, % of total NCF

The trap hiding in this example: a 2,059% contribution from NCFO sounds absurd — until you notice why it's so large. Total NCF (the denominator) was only 544 in 2024, while gross operating cash flow was 11,204. When the bottom-line total is small relative to the gross flows, vertical % structure gets stretched into extreme numbers even though nothing unusual happened operationally. Exam-ready point: vertical analysis of cash flow structure should always be read together with the absolute figures (5.2), not on its own — otherwise a healthy company with a small net cash change can look statistically alarming.

Quick reference

Formula Sheet

Everything you need to compute and interpret in under a minute, the night before the exam.

Ability to pay debt
NOCF ÷ Total Debt
≥ 1 → cash generated covers principal due (room to borrow more). < 1 → insufficient; should reduce borrowing.
Memory hook: "can operations alone pay off everyone I owe?"
Ability to pay interest expense
NOCF ÷ Interest Expense
≥ 3 → debt is being used efficiently, flexibility to borrow more. < 3 → borrowed funds used less efficiently; should deleverage.
Memory hook: cash should comfortably out-earn its own interest bill.
Ability to distribute EAT
NOCF ÷ EAT
≥ 1 → can fully fund dividends from operating cash. < 1 → operating cash alone can't cover the profit being distributed.
Memory hook: "is the dividend backed by real cash, or by paper profit?"
Ability to pay due long-term debt
NOCF ÷ Due Long-Term Debt
≥ 1 → sufficient capacity to settle maturing long-term debt. < 1 → must raise cash via investing or financing activities instead.
Memory hook: if this fails, the company is forced to sell assets or raise new financing just to survive a maturity.
